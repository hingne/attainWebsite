If you are seriously considering building a website for your company or organization, you will need to know the basics of commercial web design. When you interview prospective web design firms, make sure they truly understand the following principles, and can tell how they plan to incorporate them into the design process.

1) Understand the business before you plan a website: A genuine understanding of the client's business underlies good website design. A website has the potential to improve your business efficiency in many different ways: reducing postage and printing costs, improving organizational transparency, cultivating new leads, moving paper-based application processes to the electronic process, and many others. Take time to evaluate your business processes and the benefits and liabilities of moving each one to an Internet based process. By conducting a serious business model review in partnership with a website developer, you will find multiple uses for a website that will benefit your company and your site's visitors.

2) Don't build a website for your company, build it for your website's visitors: Website visitors are selfish. Generally, they don't care about the 'President's Message' or your 'Mission Statement'. When evaluating whether to bring business processes online, also evaluate how it will affect your visitors' experience. A website should have functionality and information that addresses the wants of your visitors. If you fail to provide a quick way to find what their looking for, or worse, are missing obvious features or information, you'll lose a visitor as fast as it takes her to hit the 'Back' button. Anticipate--or better yet, ask--what features your customers want to see on your website.

Meet your visitors needs, and they will trust your company and turn to your website as a resource.




3) Understand the novice user, and design for him: It is vital that your website is usable by the least experienced visitor that might visit your website. The design should be easy to navigate, uncluttered, easy to read, and shouldn't require the latest, greatest computer hardware. A good exercise before designing your website is to create a profile of your primary, secondary, and tertiary audiences. You can use generalities and your website logs (if you have access to them) to create your profiles.

4) An Internet marketing strategy has to compliment a company's offline marketing strategy: More and more, marketing departments are becoming valued contributors to a website's construction; gone are the days of the IT department leading the construction of your website. Make no mistake, your website represents your company as strongly as your brick-and-mortar operation. If your website is outdated, lacks the proper information, or is inconsistent with your offline marketing strategy, visitors will be frustrated and question not only your website, but your company.

5) Set goals, and choose measurement tools to determine how close you are to meeting them: Success can only be determined if you have a goal from the outset of the project. Do you want to gain more leads? Are you working on building an email list? Do you want to sell 40% more widgets than you did last year? Are you trying to offset offline costs by pushing clients to use your online services? Determine the goals so both your web design firm and you can plan for success. Once the website is launched, measure your success by anecdotal and empirical data (email, comments from associates, web tracking software). These days, there are sophisticated website tracking packages that can detail the exact movements of visitors and the success or failure of your visitors in reaching your 'goal pages'. This software can also help your company refine your online strategy, helping you provide a better online experience to your visitors.

Remember, your website is another facet of your business. Like any employee, your website should represent your company in the best manner possible. It should be informative, well-groomed, easy to work with, and effective in meeting the needs of potential and present clients. Set goals for your website as you would for an employee and measure its productivity.
